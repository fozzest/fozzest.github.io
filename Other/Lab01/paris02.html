Walls aren't just barriers. They facilitate unique communites, events, and social interactions. Nebuchadnezzar II (634-562 BCE) built three walls around Babylon at heights of forty feet and so broad at the top that chariots could race around them.

Through my research I found that techniques for breaching walls have evolved overtime, but moreso perhaps reflect the shifting climate and evironments that surround them. For example, Louis XIV tore down the old Charles V walls in the 17th century, opening up Paris, whist simultaneously building a complex systme of defenses along the lowland front of France that played into the development of migrations and wars for centuries to come. Children of these defensive structures eventuated in the German invasion of Belgium as a way of circumventing the fortifications. In the US, the infamous barrier with Mexico ranges from developed gates and checkpoints to simple expanses of desert.

Thus, how walls are maintained, and what they close out, but also what they don't intend to shift.

For this project, I wanted to craft an experience that engages publically, and speaks to the efforts to breach walls and form communities throughout time.
