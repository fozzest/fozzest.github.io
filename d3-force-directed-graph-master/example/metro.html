Background Research Paris Metro as Constellations

Lines vs Networks

The Paris metro is a tapestry of lines, being built over time, on top of one another over the past hundred years. Becasue of this, the system is similar across lines, but not consistent and in concert with one another. The space between metro stations also varries greatly; with some stations being over 500m away from one another despite being on the same line.

One of the most fascinating results from this evolving network of lines is that sometimes travelling on one line is not technically the closest or fastest way to travel between points. Lines like the 2 or 6 circumvent the city, crossing over the same line multiple times. One of the explorations in this project is to see the lines and sections that create more efficient networks than a single line.

I wanted to create a system that used proximity - in this case the amount of stations between points - as opposed to physical distance or the name of the line a station was on.

Grid Systems

Because of the notion that lines might not be as efficient as networks, I wanted to remove the hierarchy that lines take over network proximity. To do this, I drafted a grid system in Sketch that used equilateral triangles to distribute stations objectivley, equally spaced, without the influence of chained lines.

By doing this, stations are chained together by the number of stops between them, as opposed to physical geography, or line name.

Data for Play

The exploratory portion of this project has been an exercise in exploring data through code. By trialing different functions and layouts, I have tried to not only emulate the diagrams drawn above, but have been informed by how the data behves to make new decisions and initiatives.

D3js

D3js is a Javascript library that makes it easier to visually graph data and relationships. The techniques used in my graphs are known as force-directed layouts, where sources and levels create networks between data points.
